## 1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

MealEase has 27 published blog posts across 4 categories: **Decision Fatigue**, **Daily Problem**, **Quick Meals**, and **Family & Kids**. The content is well-written and honest, but has three structural weaknesses that are suppressing organic traffic:

1. **Thin topical authority** — 27 posts spread across too many micro-topics with no cluster depth. Google rewards sites that own a topic, not sites that touch it.
2. **Missing high-volume keywords** — The biggest search terms in the dinner-planning space (meal planning apps, weekly dinner planner, what to cook this week) are not targeted.
3. **Weak conversion architecture** — CTAs are generic and not tied to the specific pain point of each post. A reader who just searched "picky eater dinner ideas" needs a different CTA than one who searched "cheap family dinners."

**The opportunity:** The "busy family dinner" space is dominated by recipe sites (AllRecipes, Delish, Budget Bytes) that are not optimized for the *decision* layer. MealEase's angle — removing the decision, not just providing recipes — is a genuine differentiator that can rank for high-intent, low-competition queries.

**Target:** 50,000 monthly organic sessions within 6 months; 500+ signups/month from organic by month 9.

### 2.2 Audit Findings — What Every Post Is Missing

**Universal gaps across all 27 posts:**

1. **Word count is too thin** — Every post is ~400–600 words. Google's top-ranking family dinner content averages 1,200–2,000 words. Posts need to be 2–3× longer with more depth, examples, and supporting detail.
2. **No H2/H3 hierarchy beyond sections** — The `BlogSection.heading` maps to H2, but there are no H3 subheadings within sections. This limits featured snippet eligibility.
3. **No actual recipes** — Posts describe dinner formats but never give a complete recipe. Recipe-adjacent content (ingredient lists, timing, serving notes) dramatically increases dwell time and reduces bounce rate.
4. **No images beyond hero** — Single hero image per post. Top-ranking dinner content has 3–6 images (step photos, finished dish, ingredient layout).
5. **No author E-E-A-T signals** — `DEFAULT_AUTHOR` is "MealEase Editorial / Family nutrition team." No bio, no credentials, no photo. Google's Helpful Content system penalizes anonymous health/nutrition content.
6. **No `updatedAt` date on most posts** — All posts show `publishedAt: '2026-04-14'` to `'2026-04-16'` with no `updatedAt`. Freshness signals matter for evergreen content.
7. **Internal links point to non-existent posts** — Several posts link to slugs that don't exist yet: `/blog/the-5-meal-rotation-that-ended-our-dinner-chaos`, `/blog/dinner-templates-that-end-decision-fatigue-forever`, `/blog/pantry-meal-ideas-when-payday-is-still-days-away`, `/blog/why-you-hate-deciding-whats-for-dinner`, `/blog/school-night-dinners-kids-ask-for-again`, `/blog/cheap-dinner-ideas-that-dont-taste-cheap`, `/blog/how-to-stop-wasting-food-and-money-every-week`. **These broken internal links are actively hurting crawlability.** Fix or create these posts immediately.
8. **No FAQ schema on 18 of 27 posts** — Only 9 posts have FAQ sections. FAQ schema earns rich results and increases CTR by 20–30%.
9. **Tags are too narrow** — Most posts have 3 tags. Expanding to 6–8 tags per post increases topical relevance signals.
10. **No "last updated" visible to users** — Even if `updatedAt` is added to the data, it needs to render visibly on the page for trust signals.
